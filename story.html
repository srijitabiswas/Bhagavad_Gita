The Story of
Kurukshetra

Before there was wisdom, there was war. Before the teaching, a crisis. Before liberation, grief. The Bhagavad Gita does not begin in a temple or a forest — it begins on a battlefield at dawn.

"O Sanjaya, assembled in the field of dharma, the field of Kurukshetra — what did they do?"

Dhritarashtra · Bhagavad Gita 1.1 · The first words

01

The Two Armies
Assemble at Dawn

On the vast plain of Kurukshetra — a sacred field where the Pandavas' ancestors had established righteousness — two enormous armies have gathered. It is the eighteenth day of preparation. The air smells of dust and iron and incense.

On one side: the Pandavas, five princes who were robbed of their kingdom by their cousins. On the other: the Kauravas, led by the proud and jealous Duryodhana, who refused every attempt at peace.

Symbolic Meaning

Kurukshetra represents the battlefield of life itself — the field where every person must face the armies of their own attachments, fears, and desires.

अर्जुन
02

Arjuna Rides
Between the Armies

Arjuna — the greatest archer of his age, winner of a thousand battles, beloved of the gods — asks Krishna to drive his white chariot between the two armies so he can see those he must fight.

He looks. And what he sees breaks something open inside him. Not enemies. Not abstractions. Faces he knows: his grandfather Bhishma, who taught him to walk. His teacher Drona, who taught him the bow. Brothers. Uncles. Friends. Men he loves.

"Seeing them, O Krishna, my limbs fail, my mouth is parched, my body quivers, and my hair stands on end."

Arjuna · Bhagavad Gita 1.29
The Human Moment

Every great decision in life requires us to stand between two forces. Arjuna's chariot between the armies is every human being suspended between duty and love, between what is right and what is comfortable.

03

The Greatest Warrior
Falls Apart

Arjuna's legs give way. His famous bow — Gandiva, the divine weapon — slips from his fingers. His skin burns. His mind spins. And then he says something that no one expected from the warrior-hero of the age:

"I will not fight."

Arjuna · Bhagavad Gita 2.9 · The declaration that changed everything

The greatest warrior in the world sits down on the floor of his chariot, overcome. And this moment — this total breakdown — is not the end. It is the very beginning of wisdom.

Why This Matters

Arjuna's collapse is not weakness — it is the crisis that makes wisdom possible. Only when our usual strategies fail us do we become truly open to transformation. The Gita honours this breakdown.

कृष्ण
04

The Teacher
Begins to Speak

Krishna — charioteer, friend, and the divine in human form — has waited. He has watched Arjuna's breakdown with neither impatience nor judgment. And now, in the silence after the confession, he speaks.

Not with commands. Not with rebuke. With gentle, profound wisdom addressed to a human in genuine crisis. He begins with a challenge wrapped in compassion:

"While speaking learned words, you are mourning for what is not worthy of grief. Those who are wise lament neither for the living nor for the dead."

Krishna · Bhagavad Gita 2.11 · The teaching begins

And so begins one of the greatest dialogues in all of human history. 700 verses. 18 chapters. Questions that humanity has asked for five thousand years — answered with extraordinary clarity.

The Universal Teacher

Every person who has ever felt lost, overwhelmed, or unsure of their duty is Arjuna. Every moment of sudden clarity, every insight that illuminated the path when we needed it most — that is Krishna.
